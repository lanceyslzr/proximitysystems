The Real Problem

Turnover isn't
about compensation.

Most organizations throw money at retention. Higher wages. Better benefits. Perks. And people still leave. Because the real drivers are invisible until someone walks out the door.

The True Cost of One Resignation

Lost productivity. Disrupted workflows. Increased overtime. Diminished morale. Weakened patient and client experiences. Every resignation costs far more than it looks like on paper — and it compounds with every one that follows.

Poor manager relationships

Employees don't leave companies. They leave managers. When the daily relationship between a team member and their supervisor breaks down, disengagement starts long before the resignation letter.

Feeling undervalued and unheard

Recognition isn't about awards. It's daily acknowledgement that someone's work matters. When people feel invisible, they stop investing and start looking.

Lack of clarity and purpose

Without understanding how their role connects to something meaningful, employees drift. Purpose drives retention more than any perk in every sector we work in.

Erosion of trust over time

Trust breaks slowly, then all at once. Inconsistent communication, unmet promises, and micromanagement compound into a resignation no one saw coming.

Excessive workload and burnout

When staff shortages create chronic overload, your best people carry the weight until they can't. Burnout exits accelerate the very cycle you're trying to stop.
